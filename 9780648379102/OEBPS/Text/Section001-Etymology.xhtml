Significance of the surname

Scottish

The usage surnames came about for a number of logical and logistical reasons. A classical reasoning is the recognition of binding people to form some type of paternal allegiance for the purpose of security and prosperity. For the ability of someone to identify their ally in a form of verbal recognition. In the Scottish clans system a designated leader would cry their surname and a slogan, then their family members would gather around the 'heidsman' and take orders from them in battle. For this reason one needs to be able to identify a person or grouping of people as either an ally or enemy in the same manner as a flag or a banner would symbolise visually. thus a surname is a form of protection and security, of reassurance and valour.

Anglo-Saxon

Tradition from the continent was to draw lots to determine who would lead their people into battle. the Anglo-Saxon powerhouseinf Britain was in the southern parts whence the kings lived, but the Angles in Northumberland, when they began to settle down, they formed their own kings for a time until the Danes destroyed their kingdom. thus Anglic 'graynes', or family clans, developed in the same manor as the Highland Scottish clans in which they were in contact with. the clan styled warfare was an effective system to deal with blitzing raids and guerrillas warfare that not only sustained the north against the Roman Empire but pushed the empire's borders back. Here, collectively, people gathered under unique family names along the border and drove an Empire back. Saint Germanus of Auxerre empowered the Britons with a unison voice with the word ''hallelujah'' and so the enemy thence flee. 1.

Norman

Controlled England utilised surnames for a completely a different reason, Taxation. the written form of recording surnames would have more to do with taxing the inhabitants. Hereditary surnames were officially adopted in Britain by Henry VIII. By the 14th century most English and Scottish people had acquired surnames, but many Scottish and Welsh people did not adopt surnames until the 17th century, or even later.

Surnames of Northumbria

Routledge/Rutledge

Tribal origin

''The Rutledge surname is derived from a grayne of Anglic folk from the ancient kingdom of Northumbria, whom dwelt between the rivers Tweed and the Tyne''.

Surname origin

Whence the geographical feature describe the colouring Red 'Rut' pertaining to the bed of the pools 'laug' with the additional suffix -ís or -es.

Timeline

First recorded in 1447 in connection with Symon Routlage of Hawick, Scotland (the Scotts of Buccleuch). the surname later became most numerous in the English county of Cumberland, there first recorded in England in 1478 when Cuthbert and John Routlege were sworn in by King Richard III at Carlisle and thence granted the lands of Bewcastle. the surname today is found predominantly throughout the English speaking countries of North America, Australia, Canada, England , Ireland, New Zealand, Scotland, and South Africa.
